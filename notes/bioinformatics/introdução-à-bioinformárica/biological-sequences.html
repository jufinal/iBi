Polymers

Definition

“Polymers are very large molecules made of repeating patterns of small molecules, called monomers. Many polymers are made by repeating the same small molecule over and over again. Others are made from two monomers linked in a pattern.”
Source

 

 

Monomer Nature

  • Sugars, e.g cellulose
  • Amino-acids, e.g. proteins
  • Nucleotides, e.g. nucleic acids

Biological Function

  • Structural, e.g. proteins, cellulose
  • Enzymatic Activity, e.g. proteins
  • Storage, e.g. glycogen
  • Information Transmission, e.g. RNA, proteins
  • Information Storage, e.g. nucleic acids

Structure & Organization

  • Linear
    • Sequence of residues
  • Branched
  • Network

Sequence versus Polymer

  • The polymer is an existing substance with properties, structure and possible roles in the cell or otherwise
    • The sequence is a limited representation of the existing molecule.
    • Only the sequential order of the residues is portrayed.
      • Implications of that sequential order are up to the reader of the sequence.
  • The way residues are represented depends on the intended use or volition of the sequence coder.

Sequence versus Information

  • The sequence as an information container.
    • The sequential order of residues is the information contained
  • Other features conveying information
    • Modifications to the residues
    • Molecule folding
    • Association with other concomitant molecules
  • How to measure the information content in a naive approach?
    • Deviation to a random result
    • Looking at the sequence as a sequence of "choices" between available states
    • Assessing the degree of "organization" conveyed by the sequence
  • Information content varies throughout gene expression

Writing or Editing Sequences

  • Sequences are usually recorded manually as a plain text through a regular text editor

  • Software also records sequences as output
    • As no human is involved, formats can be more complex
  • Fluorograms resulting from sequencing DNA can also be regarded as a form of sequence recording

Sequence Production

  • Biopolymer sequencing
    • DNA versus Protein
      • Completely different analytical concepts
        • Residue position through template
        • Residue chemical properties
    • DNA Sequencing (~50 years record)
      • Methodology
        • Chemical - Maxam & Gilbert
        • Enzymatic - Sanger
          • Conceptual foundation for most of the subsequent developments
          • PCR revolution (~40 years record)
            • Increased agility
          • Robotization
        • Next Generation Sequencing (NGS)
          • Read Assembly

            • The process limiting step
            • Higher parallelization => shorter reads
            • Reads are the building blocks in Genome Assembly

              • Read
              • Contig
              • Scaffold
              • Chromosome
    • Protein Sequencing (~70years record)
      • Direct sequencing
        • Classical methods
          • Work intensive
          • Not amenable to automatisation
        • Mass spectroscopy based methods
          • Based on peptide signature recognition
            • "Black Box"
            • Not completely deterministic
      • Conceptual Translation Inference
        • Genomic sequence annotation
        • Automatic translation of inferred coding sequences (CDS)
        • Abundant source for debatable protein sequences
          • Most available protein sequences generated through this process
