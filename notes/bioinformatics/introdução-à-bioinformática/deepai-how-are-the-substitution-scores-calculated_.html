How are the substitution scores calculated?

Deep AI:

"Substitution scores in a substitution matrix are calculated using statistical methods that analyze evolutionary patterns observed in biological sequences. Here's a breakdown of how these scores are derived, specifically for amino acid substitution matrices such as BLOSUM and PAM:

1. Collecting Data:

  • Sequence Alignments: The process begins with a large dataset of aligned protein sequences. These sequences can come from various sources, including homologous sequences identified through sequence similarity searches (e.g., BLAST) or conserved domains.
  • Evolutionary Relationships: The selected sequences should represent homologous proteins across different species, allowing the analysis of evolutionary changes over time.

2. Counting Substitutions:

  • Observation of Substitutions: As the sequences are aligned, the frequency of each amino acid substitution can be recorded. This means counting how often specific amino acids occur in a pairwise manner within the aligned sequences.
  • Example: If in a set of aligned sequences, alanine (A) is found 100 times, arginine (R) is found 50 times, and the substitution A to R occurs 5 times, you would record that information.

3. Calculating Probabilities:

  • Probability of Substitution: Calculate the observed frequency of each substitution by dividing the count of each substitution by the total number of aligned pairs (note that the total pairs can be related to the number of aligned residues, typically adjusted to account for the total occurrences of each specific amino acid).
  • Background Frequencies: Also calculate the frequency of occurrence of each amino acid in the dataset. For example, if alanine appears in 10% of the sequences, its background frequency is 0.10.

4. Calculating Scores:

  • Log-Odds Ratios: Most substitution matrices, such as BLOSUM and PAM, use a log-odds scoring system to derive substitution scores. The score for substituting amino acid ( i ) for amino acid ( j ) can be calculated as follows:

    • S(i, j) is the score for substituting i with j .
    • P(i j) is the observed probability of substitution from i to j .
    • P(i) and P(j) are the background frequencies of i and j respectively.

  • Handling Rare Substitutions: If a substitution is not observed frequently, it may be assigned a negative or even zero score to reflect its rarity.

5. Threshold Settings:

  • BLOSUM (Block Substitution Matrix): The BLOSUM series focuses on conserved blocks of sequences from different proteins. A specific threshold (like BLOSUM62) determines how similar sequences need to be before they are considered in the matrix's calculations. BLOSUM62, for example, is derived from sequences that are at least 62% identical.

  • PAM (Point Accepted Mutation): PAM matrices are based on accepted point mutations, where each matrix entry PAM(n) characterizes the likelihood of mutations after n mutations. It reflects evolutionary distance.

6. Normalization and Finalizing the Matrix:

  • Normalization: Scores may be normalized to ensure that the matrix is appropriate for the type of sequence analysis being carried out.
  • Final Adjustments: After scoring, the matrix may undergo adjustments and validation to ensure it accurately reflects biological realities, and changes may be made based on empirical data or expert input in the field of sequence alignment.

Summary:

Substitution scores are calculated from empirical data derived from aligned sequences, utilizing observed frequencies of substitutions, background frequencies, and log-odds ratios. The resulting matrix provides a basis for assessing the relative likelihood of amino acid or nucleotide substitutions during evolutionary processes."

Comment

The text of the answer is generally on the right side even if the employed terms range from the casual to the improper. Each and every sequence aligned to be used in the matrix building process has to be presumed as homologous. Their presumption to be homologous id usually grounded on the Similarity Criterion, and this must involve global alignment, so no BLAST comparisons here. Moreover the sequence group should be monophyletic or at least paraphyletic.
Regarding substitutions and their frequency, the dichotomy should be between more frequent than expected and its opposite, the former are represented by positive numbers and the latter by negative ones. So talking about "a negative or even zero score" is a perfect nonsense.
Vocabulary fluidity is dangerous as "likelihood" is not a perfect synonym to probability, and "mutation" has the same problems if used as substitution. This volubility in the use of terms may reflect the heterogeneity of the sources used to train the model.
The description of either "thresholds" or PAM matrices is close to nil.
