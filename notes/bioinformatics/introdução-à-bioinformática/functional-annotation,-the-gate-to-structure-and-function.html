Proteins, a Modular Evolution

In Principio

  • Domain (Wetlaufer 1973), the evolution unit

    • Source: PMC
      .

    • Definition

      "a region of a protein's polypeptide chain that is self-stabilizing and that folds independently from the rest. Each domain forms a compact folded three-dimensional structure. Many proteins consist of several domains, and a domain may appear in a variety of different proteins. Molecular evolution uses domains as building blocks and these may be recombined in different arrangements to create proteins with different functions. In general, domains vary in length from between about 50 amino acids up to 250 amino acids in length."

      Source: Wikipedia
      .

    • Main features
      • Self-folding units
      • Discrete evolution units
      • Architecture recombination
        • Novel tertiary structures & functions


    • .

    • Domain Architecture
      • Individual domains may occur in different quantities and/or combinations
        • New specificities or new functions


      • .

  • Family, a rather ill-defined evolutionary unit
    • Several authors & different sets of criteria
      • High context dependence
      • Some sequence conservation
        • At least 30 to 35% shared identity
      • Tertiary structure conservation
        • Noticeable
      • Assumed common ancestor
        • Evolution driven
    • An expedient and at times unsound concept.


    • .

  • Superfamily (Dayhoff el al. 1974, Dayhoff et al. 1975)


    • .

    • Definition

      "the largest grouping (clade) of proteins for which common ancestry can be inferred (see homology). Usually this common ancestry is inferred from structural alignment and mechanistic similarity, even if no sequence similarity is evident. Sequence homology can then be deduced even if not apparent (due to low sequence similarity). Superfamilies typically contain several protein families which show sequence similarity within each family."

      Source: Wikipedia
      .

    • They represent a deep clade with a broad range of diversity.
      • A shared common ancestor
      • Homology assessed through folding similarity
        • The sequence is hardly conserved, but it is also considered in conjunction with structure
          • Function may vary due to the amount of evolution included in the group
        • Delimitation is critical to avoid the contamination with homoplasy


    • .

  • Motifs

    • Definition

      "a short conserved sequence pattern associated with distinct functions of a protein or DNA. It is often associated with a distinct structural site performing a particular function."

      Source: Xiong 2006
      .

    • The most conserved subsequence for any domain
      • The closest to a consensus sequence even if consensus is seldom at hand
      • Presumed association with the core of a domain
        • e.g. active center for a catalytic domain
      • Several may have been recorded for certain domains
    • Short by nature
      • l = 10..20 amino acids
      • Domain + motif


        .
        Source: PROSITE
        .

    • Motifs versus Domains


    • .

    • Case Study
      • PROSITE Patterns (Bairoch 1992)
        • Single motifs coded as regular expressions
          • Code Meaning
            - Site separator
            <A..Z> Defined residue
            x Undefined residue
            [...] Allowed residues for a site
            {...} Non-allowed residues
            <something>* Arbitrary repetition of
            (n) Fixed repetition (n times)
        • Matches detected by a Finite State Automata Algorithm (McCulloch & Pitts 1943)


          • .









          • .

          • Only perfect matches are detected
            • All-or-nothing approach
              • Not amenable to statistical testing
            • Novel elements recruitment require the regex rebuild
      • Diffuse Regular Expressions (the now defunct eMOTIF)
        • Motifs coded as regular expressions
        • Instead of observed residues
          • The whole group of amino acids, regarding properties, was included
        • Extreme ambiguity results
          • Novel members recruitment possible
          • Too many false positives to be useful
      • PRINTS, motifs fingerprint
        • Domains coded as a set of multiple motifs
          • Higher specificity
          • A step towards the coding/detection of the whole domain


          • .

  • Domains
    • The whole evolutionary unit
      • l = 50..250 (or more) amino acids
      • No expectation for consensus
    • Case Studies
      • The Gribskov Profile / PSSM (NCBI CDD)
        • Scoring matrix
          • For each residue in any site
            • Along the the domain
          • Built on the set of known elements for the domain
            • Regular recoding to integrate the novel elements

        • Can be aligned to a given sequence
        • Sensitive & Fast
          • May be built on a small set of sequences
          • Integer based arithmetics
            • Small RAM footprint
            • Fast calculations
        • Allows for statistical testing


        • .

      • Hidden Markov Models (Bucher & Bairoch 1994, Sonnhammer & Kahn 1994)
        • Confusion Matrix
          • Describes site-specific probabilities for
            • Matching the observed residue
            • Site deletion
            • Insertion of a new site


            • .

        • Higher sensitivity than PSSM-based detection
          • Large training set required
          • Heavier computing requirements
          • Sites have specific weights, and their relevance varies
          • Motifscan output
